# History & Beliefs

Use this page for the layer of history and belief that helps you make a character: what people remember, which places carry weight, and what ordinary customs might shape a family, crew, or hometown.

This is player-facing orientation, not a full lore encyclopedia. Some old ruins, taboo routes, hidden waters, and Caldera questions are intentionally left unresolved so they can matter in play.

## What People Remember

Emberwreath is a volcanic archipelago, often called **the Wreath**. No single crown, temple, guild, species, or island rules all of it. Its peoples began mostly on separate homeland islands, then became connected by crossings, rescue, trade, marriage, work, pilgrimage, and necessity.

Memory is uneven across the islands. Some communities keep long settlement records, named groves, measured reservoirs, or old boundary markers. Others remember through songs, route warnings, family obligations, wreck stories, seasonal work, or rituals around water, food, fire, and the dead.

The islands also differ in how deeply they have been shaped by post-contact life. A homeland village, a mixed port district, a reef guide family, a canal crew, and a frontier work camp may all tell Emberwreath's history differently. For character creation, that means your origin can be old-rooted, recently mixed, route-based, displaced, skeptical, devout, or practical without needing one official version of the past.

## The Shape of the Wreath

The islands form loose inner and outer crescents around the central Caldera. The inner islands sit closer to the volcanic danger and shorter inter-island routes. The outer crescent, reef regions, and frontier routes pull people across greater distances, where weather, pilots, repair yards, safe water, and trustworthy harbors matter.

Seafaring contact made the Wreath culturally connected, but not culturally identical. Trade routes carry more than cargo: they carry marriages, debts, words, festivals, taboos, tools, rumors, saints, grudges, and practical habits.

**Breakwater** matters because it is a major cosmopolitan harbor, repair yard, warehouse island, and outer-route anchor. It does not rule Emberwreath. It matters because ships stop there, crews change there, goods are translated there, and people from many islands learn how much they depend on strangers.

## Sacred Geography

In Emberwreath, belief is often tied to place. A sacred place may be a temple, but it may also be a reservoir gate, old road, reef passage, named grove, caldera overlook, forbidden ruin, family grave, harbor marker, freshwater lens, storm shelter, terrace, route cairn, or wreck no one salvages.

Some places are sacred because they sustain life. Some are sacred because the dead are remembered there. Some are sacred because a warning was once ignored. Some are sacred because people are still alive only because an older generation marked a boundary and taught others to respect it.

Characters do not need secret knowledge to care about these places. A character might know only the public rule: do not cut from that grove, do not dive that blue hole, do not move that road marker, do not take water before the measure is called, do not boast about that passage, do not enter that ruin for proof.

## Belief in Practice

Most everyday belief in Emberwreath is practical before it is theological. People make offerings, keep taboos, maintain markers, remember ancestors, respect route closures, and argue about what the living owe to the dead.

Common practices might include:

- leaving a small offering before a voyage, harvest, repair, hunt, crossing, or descent underground;
- honoring water rules in dry places, reservoir towns, atolls, and shipboard life;
- treating food measures, granary duties, and road access as moral issues, not just logistics;
- listening to storm signs, volcanic warnings, closed tides, and old route markers;
- preserving named groves, hidden gardens, wells, terraces, wreck memories, or family tools;
- refusing to salvage certain wrecks, enter forbidden ruins, poison water, waste stores, or mock the dead.

A character can be devout, casual, skeptical, syncretic, or shaped by a custom they do not personally believe. In play, beliefs are most useful when they create choices: what you refuse to do, what you feel obligated to protect, whose warning you trust, and what your family taught you never to ignore.

Use any of these prompts to add a little history or belief to a character without writing a full doctrine.

  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>Taboo</h2>
    <p>What taboo were you raised to respect? Is it about water, fire, food, routes, names, wrecks, ruins, ancestors, harvest, storms, or taking more than you are owed?</p>
  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>Family memory</h2>

  </section>

  <section class="card">
    <h2>Warnings</h2>
