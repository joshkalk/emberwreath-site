# Brief History of Emberwreath

Emberwreath is a volcanic archipelago. Sailors often call it the Wreath, while older poems and religious traditions sometimes call it the Broken Wreath. No single crown, temple, guild, species, or island rules all of it.

Use this page for a broad player-facing summary, not a full encyclopedia.

## Before the crossings

For much of remembered history, the nine playable peoples developed mostly separately on homeland islands. Humans were rooted in Breadbasket, Zivari in Saffra, Veyr in Sunspire, Ferren in Greenwood, Aumari in the Tidefall Atolls, Axali in Hollowdeep, Scolen in Caelmere, Kelden in Ashspine, and Thryss in Veilgarden.

Those homelands shaped practical life and sacred memory. Food, water, timber, medicine, routes, warning signs, hidden reserves, volcanic resources, and guarded knowledge all became matters of survival as much as identity.

## The first crossings

Around 500 years ago, contact began through scattered crossings, storms, trade, rescue, curiosity, and survival. These early meetings were uneven. Some became friendships, marriages, exchanges, and shared techniques; others brought fear, debt, rivalry, or old injuries that families still remember.

The crossings did not erase homeland traditions. They made people aware that the sea between homes could be dangerous, sacred, useful, and politically important.

## The trade age

Over time, crossings became routes. Routes became ports, laws, trade, obligations, shrines, and shared systems. Emberwreath became interconnected, but not equal or unified.

Breadbasket's food, Caelmere's waterworks, Greenwood's timber, Tidefall's navigation, Hollowdeep's hidden reserves, Saffra's medicines and luxury crops, Ashspine's volcanic resources, Veilgarden's guarded knowledge, and Sunspire's route warnings all matter. That interdependence creates cooperation, but also leverage, resentment, and pressure.

## The eruption 60 years ago

About 60 years ago, a major Caldera eruption darkened seas, damaged routes, strained crops and water systems, moved refugees, and exposed how dependent the islands had become on one another.

Most people alive now were born after the eruption. Most elders remember it. For many families, the eruption is not ancient history; it is a remembered break between the world their elders knew and the world they inherited.

## Emberwreath today

The world has recovered, but its systems remain brittle. Food, water, timber, shipping, medicine, route warnings, religious obligations, and emergency reserves are practical concerns and political arguments.

Present-day Emberwreath is rebuilt, busy, sacred, practical, scarred, and not fully settled. Characters grow up in a Wreath that works again, but almost everyone knows it works because people maintain fragile routes, honor warnings, and depend on islands that may not fully trust one another.
