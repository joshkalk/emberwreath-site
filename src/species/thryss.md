# Thryss

*The terrace drinks before the field does. The field drinks before the house does. The house drinks before I do. That is not sacrifice. That is how anything survives here.*

## At a glance

- **Homeland:** Veilgarden.
- **Known for:** high fog valleys, moisture-storing plant bodies, mist-crowns, fog terraces, elder memory, careful agriculture, route guidance, and patient obligations.
- **Common places to start:** mist valleys, cliff pockets, sheltered terraces, damp stone basins, fog-catching frames, root courts, seedling terraces, and Veilgarden's coastal port.
- **Character hooks:** Lost seed record seeker, blight investigator, missing-relative searcher, trade debt carrier, fragile shipment escort, elder promise bearer, fog guide, harvest counter, medicine grower, or restless valley youth.

## Rooted Beneath the Mist

The Thryss are long-lived plant-bodied people shaped by the high fog valleys of Veilgarden. Their bodies are waxy, fibrous, moisture-storing, and mobile, closer to fog-fed succulents, reeds, and root-crowned terrace plants than to trees or flowers. They are people with tools, households, debts, tempers, professions, and bodies that must be tended with the same care they give the land.

Thryss life is built around fragile abundance. Veilgarden is livable because generations have caught fog, repaired terraces, guarded springs, planted carefully, and taught children how to survive where a careless season can undo a family’s work. A Thryss adventurer carries that inheritance into the wider archipelago, whether as duty, exile, curiosity, scholarship, or proof that patience can still act.

## Waxy Cores and Mist-Crowns

A Thryss body is organized around a bulbous living core that holds their organs, moisture-storing tissues, digestive structures, face, and resting roots. They have no humanoid neck, shoulders, chest, or abdomen. Their offshoots grow from the core itself, giving them a silhouette unlike a walking tree, vine-creature, or person in plantlike skin.

Their outer tissue is waxy and fibrous, with layered strands beneath tougher surface growth. Most Thryss are colored in muted volcanic and wet-stone tones: ash-brown, gray-green, smoky blue, dusty olive, rust, dull red, pale yellow, lichen green, or weathered gray. Their surfaces may be smooth, ridged, segmented, or corded with fibers.

Their face sits in the forward upper surface of the core. It is minimal and embedded, with small dark seedlike eyes, no nose, and a subtle mouth fold connected to internal breathing and digestive chambers. Other peoples often find Thryss expressions difficult to read until they learn the small shifts of eye, core, offshoot, and mist-crown.

Thryss can draw trace moisture and nutrients through resting roots, but they still eat cultivated fruits, succulents, insects, preserved foods, fish, meat, and imported staples.

Four walking offshoots emerge low from the core, rising outward before arching down to the ground. Each ends in spreading rootlets that grip wet stone, moss, mud, and terrace steps. These offshoots give a Thryss a careful, stable gait rather than a humanoid stride. Two grasping offshoots emerge higher on the core and serve as their primary manipulators for tools, weapons, writing, farming, cooking, instruments, and spellcasting. They have no hands or fingers. Their grip depends on curling, wrapping, bracing, and fibrous pressure.

A Thryss’s upper growth is called a mist-crown. It is a dense crown of trained fibers, reedlike strands, and moisture-catching growths, not hair. Young Thryss shape their crowns over time, and older Thryss often bear forms that show family habit, valley tradition, profession, or personal taste.

When sitting, sleeping, or resting, a Thryss settles their core against the ground or another supporting surface. Fine resting roots emerge from the underside of the core and press into damp stone, moss, soil, living vegetation, fertile earth, or fog-wet ground to drink, anchor, and recover.

## Clothing and Equipment

Thryss clothing is built around a central core rather than a humanoid torso. Most wear fitted harnesses, cord belts, hanging panels, tool loops, offshoot guards, mist-crown ties, and weather screens. These leave the face, offshoots, and resting-root surfaces clear.

Mountain Thryss favor practical gear: seed tubes, climbing fittings, harvest tools, guide cords, ledger cases, and field markers. Thryss living elsewhere add imported fabrics, shell fasteners, metal clips, dyed panels, or local badges adapted to their bodies.

Armor, clothing, packs, saddles, and other fitted gear must be adapted for a Thryss’s core, offshoots, root structures, and movement. This normally does not change cost in communities familiar with Thryss bodies, but rare, rushed, foreign, or highly specialized work may cost more at the DM’s discretion.

Ordinary boots do not fit them. Magic boots usually function for a Thryss as root guards, lower-body bands, fitted wraps, or harnessed equivalents rather than literal boots.

## Veilgarden and the Fog Terraces

Every Thryss begins life in Veilgarden’s high fog valleys. Thryss now live across the inhabited islands, but Veilgarden remains biologically and culturally central to their people.

Veilgarden’s coast is mixed, maritime, and practical, with ports, warehouses, ship repair, and neighborhoods where many peoples live and work. The mountain interior is mostly Thryss. Roads narrow into stone paths, fog thickens, and water becomes harder to find. Outsiders are not usually barred from the highlands, but few settle there without Thryss help. Springs hide, paths vanish in mist, and a familiar slope can become dangerous when the island stops showing its edges.

Thryss communities are built where fog gathers reliably: mist valleys, cliff pockets, sheltered terraces, and damp stone basins. Settlements often include fog-catching frames, terraced fields, fruit groves, medicinal beds, insect gardens, root courts, seedling terraces, and narrow paths marked by touch as much as sight.

Their agriculture is careful rather than expansive. Mountain communities grow fog-fed succulents, fruiting plants, medicinal herbs, aromatic crops, dye plants, resinous growths, and other crops suited to volcanic slopes and thin moisture. Through the coastal port, they export high-value mountain goods in measured quantities and import fabric, wax, metal tools, ceramics, glass, spices, sea goods, and luxuries the high valleys cannot easily produce.

A grower who keeps a terrace alive through a hard season may be honored as much as a warrior. Harvest counters, seed tenders, medicine growers, fog readers, route guides, and trade speakers all protect different parts of survival.

## Rooted Households and Patient Obligations

Thryss society is organized around households, terraces, remembered duties, and practical expertise. A household is not only a family dwelling. It is a chain of care: elders, adults, young, seed records, tools, planting rights, debts, repairs, and the living surfaces that sustain them.

Most major valleys are guided by Root Councils drawn from elder households and respected specialists. A council might include terrace keepers, fog readers, seed tenders, medicine growers, harvest counters, route guides, and port speakers. There is no single Thryss throne. In major crises, several Root Councils may call a temporary gathering, but authority usually returns to the valleys once the danger passes.

Thryss keep ledgers, tally marks, trade contracts, seed lists, route signs, and port documents. Still, their deepest knowledge is carried through elder memory. Elders remember which terrace cracked under a careless wall, which spring failed after a dry decade, which household kept its promise, and which bargain looked fair until the next generation paid for it.

This memory is not supernatural. It is long life, patient teaching, and the habit of treating the past as useful equipment.

## Life Cycle and Family

Thryss mature slowly, usually reaching adulthood around age 25, and commonly live 120 to 180 years. Some elders survive longer through long periods of rooted dormancy, though such dormancy is not a normal adventuring ability.

Thryss children begin as seeds planted in Veilgarden’s high fog valleys. Female Thryss may produce small seasonal flowers within or around the mist-crown. If fertilized, these flowers harden into seed pods, usually producing a single seed and only rarely two. Thryss treat flowers and seed pods as a modest part of biology, not as decoration or spectacle.

At least one parent must remain through the early years, tending the seedling until it can survive, move, eat, and learn as a child. Grandparents and extended kin often help, but they do not erase the parent’s duty. For this reason, Thryss families grow slowly despite long lives.

Off-island Thryss who wish to raise children usually return to Veilgarden for more than a brief visit. Some return eagerly, some delay for decades, and some never do.

## Adventurers

Thryss adventurers often leave home for obligations rather than glory. One may search for a lost seed record, investigate a blight, recover a missing relative, carry a trade debt, escort a fragile shipment, or answer an elder’s unfinished promise. Another may leave because a Root Council is too cautious, a family duty too narrow, or a valley memory too heavy.

Many Thryss become adventurers through practical work. Fog guides become scouts, ship pilots, or explorers. Harvest counters become quartermasters, auditors, or smugglers with excellent ledgers. Medicine growers seek rare ingredients, test cures, or hunt the source of a sickness. Port speakers become diplomats, spies, negotiators, or merchants.

Others are restless youths, exiles after household disputes, or Thryss who want to prove that patience can still move quickly when the moment requires it.

## How Other Peoples View Thryss

Other peoples often value Thryss as guides, healers, growers, record keepers, and steady negotiators. Sailors respect Thryss fog pilots. Farmers respect their ability to coax food from difficult land. Merchants trust their ledgers, though some complain that a Thryss bargain includes obligations no contract fully names.

Misunderstandings are common. Outsiders may mistake Thryss patience for passivity, their elder memory for mysticism, or their slow movement for weakness. Some assume Thryss are delicate because they need moisture, then learn that a plant body built for wet stone, lean soil, and hard seasons can be stubbornly difficult to break.

## Names

Thryss place the family or root-name first and the personal name second. Family names carry weight because lineage, planting rights, elder memory, and land obligations matter. In formal use, a Thryss may add a valley, terrace, council role, or working title.

Thryss names favor reedy, breathy, and resonant sounds: M, N, R, L, V, W, Y, S, Sh, Th, and H are common.

**Family Names:** Hethru, Marnel, Orruth, Saelvar, Thalru, Navori, Helmun, Loryth, Maevran, Velmoth, Sarneth, Ilvyr

**Personal Names:** Ishal, Havri, Maelis, Ruun, Thavi, Nareth, Halen, Aroth, Mavren, Yshen, Lorai, Senru

**Valley and Terrace Names:** Low Fog Vale, High Morrow Vale, East Mist Road, Third Mist Terrace, Pale Hollow, Rainless Gate, Stoneveil Basin, Old Root Court, South Fog Stair

**Working Titles:** Harvest Counter, Fog Reader, Terrace Keeper, Seed Tender, Medicine Grower, Route Guide, Port Speaker, Speaker for the Season

Examples include Hethru Ishal, Marnel Havri of Low Fog Vale, Orruth Maelis, Terrace Keeper, Saelvar Ruun, Speaker for the Season, or Thalru Nareth of the East Mist Road.

## Thryss Traits

Your Thryss character has traits shaped by a fog-fed mountain homeland, a waxy plant body, careful movement, resting roots, and a culture of measured survival.

- **Ability Score Increase.** Your Wisdom score increases by 2, and your Constitution score increases by 1.
- **Age.** Thryss mature slowly, reaching adulthood around age 25. They commonly live 120 to 180 years.
- **Size.** Thryss have unusual silhouettes, but most occupy roughly the same space as a human-sized person. Your size is Medium.
- **Speed.** Your base walking speed is 25 feet.
- **Creature Type.** You are a Humanoid.
- **Unusual Body Plan.** You have a bulbous central core, four walking offshoots, two grasping offshoots, a mist-crown, and fine resting roots. Your grasping offshoots allow you to use weapons, shields, tools, spellcasting focuses, instruments, and magic items normally, though your movements involve curling, wrapping, bracing, and fibrous grip rather than fingers.

  Armor, saddles, packs, and fitted clothing must be made or altered for your body. This normally does not change their cost unless the DM decides the item is rare, rushed, foreign, or unusually specialized. You cannot wear ordinary boots, though you may use adapted root guards, lower-body bands, fitted wraps, climbing fittings, or similar gear.

  Your offshoots do not grant extra attacks, extra object interactions, extra shields, extra spell components, increased reach, or extra actions unless a trait or feature explicitly says so.
- **Fog Drinker.** You require only half as much drinking water as a human. In fog, mist, steady rain, or similarly moisture-rich air, you can go twice as long without drinking before suffering the effects of dehydration.

  You have advantage on Wisdom (Survival) checks made to locate, preserve, or safely collect water from fog, dew, damp stone, hidden springs, moisture-rich plants, or natural catchments. You also have advantage on Wisdom (Survival) checks made to avoid becoming lost in fog, mist, heavy rain, or natural darkness.
- **Braced Roots.** While you are conscious and at least two walking offshoots are in contact with a solid surface, you have advantage on ability checks and saving throws made to resist being moved against your will.
- **Waxy Cuticle.** Your waxy outer tissue helps you endure dry air, thirst, and moisture loss. You have advantage on saving throws against exhaustion caused by dehydration, extreme dryness, or lack of drinking water. This trait does not grant resistance or vulnerability to fire damage.
- **Rooted Rest.** When you spend Hit Dice to regain hit points at the end of a short rest while your core is in contact with damp soil, living vegetation, moss, wet stone, fertile earth, or a similar moisture-rich natural surface, you may reroll one of those Hit Dice and use either result. You can use this trait only once per short rest.
- **Veilwise.** You gain proficiency in one of the following skills of your choice: Medicine, Nature, Stealth, or Survival.
- **Languages.** You can speak, read, and write Common and Thryss. Thryss is a breathy, rasping language with held vowels, soft clicks, and subtle mist-crown vibrations.
