# Humans

**Homeland:** Breadbasket

When the eastern granary burned, the millers did not wait for a magistrate. By sunrise, three bakeries had pooled their flour, the road crews had cleared the lower bridge, and every cart captain in Harrowbend knew which village would go hungry first. That afternoon, Mara Vell took a spear, a tally slate, and six strangers down the convoy road.

## The Weight of the Harvest

Children of Breadbasket, full granaries, busy roads, and crowded ports, humans are the most numerous and widely dispersed people in the archipelago. Their homeland is the great agricultural heart of the islands: a land of broad valleys, stepped terraces, grain fields, orchard belts, irrigation channels, mill towns, bakeries, storehouses, windbreak forests, convoy routes, and managed countryside. Breadbasket feeds far more than itself, and its people have learned to think in harvests, ledgers, roads, labor crews, debts, and hungry mouths.

Humans are not bound to Breadbasket. They live across the archipelago as farmers, sailors, soldiers, merchants, builders, scribes, scholars, settlers, diplomats, artisans, laborers, priests, smugglers, and wanderers. A human community might be a farming village beside grain fields, a crowded port district, a convoy camp on a dangerous road, a frontier hamlet, a military outpost, a merchant house, or a migrant neighborhood built by families who left debt, hunger, war, or opportunity behind them.

What marks humans most clearly is not a single custom, trade, or temperament. It is their talent for making systems large enough to outlive the people who began them. Humans build roads between valleys, store grain against famine, maintain mills, found bakeries, organize harvest crews, count taxes, guard food convoys, and argue over who has the right to plant, irrigate, grind, sell, store, or eat. At their best, they are practical, cooperative, and astonishingly adaptable. At their worst, they measure, claim, tax, schedule, and inherit before anyone else has agreed to the plan.

## Physical Description and Body Plan

Humans vary widely in height, build, complexion, hair, and features. Most stand between five and six and a half feet tall, though individuals fall outside that range. Their bodies are neither specialized for one terrain nor restricted to one kind of labor. A human might be a broad-shouldered miller, a weathered road guard, a lean sailor, a stooped clerk, a soft-handed baker, a scarred soldier, a sun-browned orchard worker, or a child of crowded streets who has never touched a plow.

This physical variety helps explain part of human success. Humans are not the strongest, swiftest, longest-lived, or best adapted people in the islands, but they can turn themselves toward many forms of work. Their bodies can farm terraces, crew ships, march roads, haul grain, keep books, mend bridges, fight in shield lines, build storehouses, and survive in settlements far from the valley where their grandparents were born.

## Clothing and Equipment

Human clothing is shaped by work, weather, rank, and travel. In Breadbasket, rural clothing often favors durable linen, wool, leather, wrapped calves, aprons, broad hats, patched cloaks, and tool belts. Millers, bakers, orchard hands, road crews, drovers, and canal workers dress for dust, flour, rain, mud, animals, and long hours. In ports and market towns, human clothing becomes more varied: layered coats, dyed sashes, counting-house vests, sailors’ wraps, convoy harnesses, soldiers’ tabards, and patched migrant garments altered to fit local custom.

Humans often carry practical equipment. A road traveler may keep a tally slate, knife, waxed food packet, spare cord, water flask, road token, and proof of passage. A farmer may carry pruning hooks, seed bags, boundary markers, or irrigation tools. A granary clerk might carry keys, seals, ledgers, and a cudgel. A convoy guard may carry a spear, shield, horn, lantern, and spare straps for wagon repairs.

Human equipment often reveals obligation. Keys mark storehouse trust. Seals mark authority. Road badges mark service. Family tools mark inheritance. A sword may belong to a crown, a village, a merchant house, or a debt that has not yet been paid.

## Homeland and Ecology

Breadbasket receives reliable rain without the overwhelming saturation found in the wettest parts of the archipelago. Its broad fertile valleys support dense settlement, massive terrace systems, orchard belts, grain fields, windbreak forests, roads, irrigation works, mills, bakeries, storehouses, and clustered villages. Few places in the islands can sustain so many people so steadily.

This abundance shaped human life. A harvest does not move itself from field to mill, from mill to bakery, from bakery to market, from market to ship, or from ship to a hungry island. Breadbasket communities became skilled at coordination: measuring stores, repairing roads, sharing labor, recording debts, settling boundary disputes, guarding routes, and moving food before storms, pirates, war, rot, or delay could stop it.

Breadbasket’s importance gives humans influence far beyond their homeland. Its grain, flour, dried fruit, pressed oils, livestock, beer, seed, and baked goods travel through ports and convoy roads across the archipelago. In wartime, Breadbasket is one of the most contested places in the world. In famine, its storehouses can become symbols of mercy, resentment, corruption, or survival.

Breadbasket’s richest valleys are also shaped by power. Old crowns, landholding families, mill-rights, granary charters, convoy obligations, village debts, irrigation claims, road wardens, tax collectors, magistrates, and port factors all press on the harvest. A king or queen may claim the grain in law, but every road, mill, storehouse, canal, village council, landholder, and convoy captain has its own interests.

Human power is useful and intrusive. A road can bring trade, medicine, bread, news, and rescue. It can also bring settlers, soldiers, surveyors, tax lists, and claims carved into stone.

## Society, Communities, and Values

Human communities often form around useful work. A village may exist because a terrace wall needs tending, a canal must be cleared, a mill needs grain, a road requires guards, or a port needs hands to load food before the weather turns. Even far from Breadbasket, human districts often gather around granaries, bakeries, warehouses, caravan yards, counting houses, barracks, shrines, workshops, or market squares.

Humans organize abundance more than ancient memory or untouched ecology. They ask how much grain remains, which road is open, whose name is on the field record, whether the bridge can hold wagons, how many mouths a storehouse can feed, and who must answer when the convoy fails to arrive. Their responsibility often feels like coordination, pressure, expansion, and hunger math.

Breadbasket is the archipelago’s clearest home for crowns and feudal obligations, but human life is not simple obedience to a throne. Landholding families bargain against tax collectors. Villages hide grain from corrupt factors. Millers fight over water flow. Convoy captains ignore foolish orders when roads become dangerous. Magistrates settle disputes that began generations before they were born. Farmers resent nobles whose charters are older than anyone’s memory. A crown may stand above the system, but the system is too large for any crown to fully own.

Not all humans are royal subjects or Breadbasket loyalists. Many are migrants, exiles, smugglers, scholars, sailors, frontier settlers, mercenaries, priests, laborers, or descendants of families who left Breadbasket long ago. Some humans carry pride in Breadbasket’s roads and granaries. Others carry bitterness toward its taxes, debts, inherited ranks, and crowded fields.

At their best, humans are practical, cooperative, adaptive, and willing to build what others only discuss. They can pool flour, clear roads, count supplies, post guards, reopen bakeries, and feed strangers because the work needs doing. At their worst, they mistake organization for permission. They can turn shared places into mapped, taxed, guarded, inherited property, then act surprised when others object.

## Life Cycle and Family Structure

Humans reach adulthood in their late teens and usually live less than a century. Their shorter lives give many human communities a sharp sense of urgency. Fields must be planted this year. Children must be trained before the next harvest. Debts must be paid before land changes hands. Roads must be repaired before the rains. A family name can rise or vanish within a few generations.

Human families take many forms across the archipelago. In Breadbasket’s villages and towns, families often connect to land, trade, debt, or service. A household may include parents, children, grandparents, apprentices, hired workers, cousins, and widowed relatives. Inheritance can bind families together or split them apart, especially where fertile land has been divided too many times. A younger child might leave for military service, scholarship, a port apprenticeship, a convoy company, or the uncertain life of an adventurer because there is no field left to inherit.

Marriage, adoption, apprenticeship, and sworn service all matter in human communities. A baker may treat an apprentice as a child. A road captain may trust a convoy crew more than kin. A landholding family may preserve bloodlines, charters, and grudges with equal care. Migrant humans often rebuild family out of neighbors, shipmates, labor gangs, or others who survived the same hard road.

## Adventurers

Humans become adventurers for practical reasons as often as romantic ones. A harvest failed. A convoy vanished. A family debt came due. A disputed field turned violent. A magistrate needed an escort. A road warden found tracks near a missing grain wagon. A miller’s child followed rumors of old ruins. A soldier finished one war and found no peace waiting at home. A scholar sought records buried in dangerous places. A displaced family needed coin, land, or a new start.

Some human adventurers leave Breadbasket because its crowded villages and inherited obligations leave little room to breathe. Others leave to serve those same obligations: protecting food shipments, negotiating trade routes, surveying new land, recovering stolen grain, guarding irrigation works, or carrying messages between settlements. Still others seek fortune, glory, knowledge, revenge, faith, or simple curiosity.

A human adventurer might be a harvest guard, convoy scout, road warden, granary clerk, former soldier, frontier settler, miller’s apprentice, baker’s child, merchant factor, displaced farmer, canal engineer, tax runner, smuggler, ruin scholar, debt collector, village champion, port laborer, runaway heir, or restless younger child with no patience for waiting their turn.

Human adventurers often carry the habits of systems with them. They count rations, ask who owns the road, check the bridge before crossing, notice empty storehouses, distrust vague promises, and understand that hunger can turn honest people dangerous.

## How Other Peoples View Humans

Other peoples often admire humans for their energy, numbers, and ability to organize work at scale. Humans can enter a damaged port and begin repairing roads, counting supplies, assigning guards, reopening bakeries, and arguing over storage rights before the dust has settled. In hungry years, many communities survive because human grain ships, mill contracts, road crews, and storehouse ledgers hold together long enough to matter.

That same confidence can make humans difficult neighbors. Other peoples may see them as restless, intrusive, or too quick to turn shared places into mapped, taxed, guarded, and inherited property. Human settlements spread. Human roads lengthen. Human magistrates ask for records. Human merchants ask who controls the mill. Human soldiers arrive to protect a convoy and remain to guard a claim.

Humans are rarely ignored. There are too many of them, moving through too many ports and valleys, building too many systems wherever they go. To some, they are the people who bring bread when the island is hungry. To others, they are the people who bring ledgers before the bread has cooled.

## Names

Human names vary more than those of many peoples. Breadbasket families often preserve names tied to settlements, trades, fields, mills, roads, orchards, granaries, or old migration routes. Humans living elsewhere may adopt local naming patterns, combine family names from several islands, or use occupational names that changed from description to inheritance over generations.

**Given Names:** Ansel, Bria, Callen, Catria, Dain, Elian, Lena, Ivo, Jory, Lysa, Mara, Noll, Petra, Rian, Thane, Willa

**Family Names:** Ashford, Barleywell, Casker, Dovemill, Fieldren, Grantham, Harrow, Kettlebrook, Millward, Orchard, Roadmere, Stonecart, Tannic, Vell, Wheatley

**Place or Trade Names:** of Harrowbend, of South Granary, Eastroad, Miller’s Gate, Orchard Row, Lower Mill, Baker’s Court, Westfield, Three Bridges, North Canal

Examples include Mara Vell, Ansel Roadmere, Bria of Lower Mill, Catria Wheatley, Jory Eastroad, or Willa Kettlebrook.

## Human Traits

Your human character has traits shaped by adaptability, broad inheritance, and life among the many peoples and pressures of the archipelago.

- **Ability Score Increase.** Each of your ability scores increases by 1.
- **Age.** Humans reach adulthood in their late teens and usually live less than a century.
- **Size.** Humans vary widely in height and build, from under 5 feet to over 6 feet tall. Your size is Medium.
- **Speed.** Your base walking speed is 30 feet.
- **Creature Type.** You are a Humanoid.
- **Languages.** You can speak, read, and write Common and one extra language of your choice. Humans often learn the languages of neighboring peoples, trade partners, military allies, rivals, or the communities among whom they were raised.
