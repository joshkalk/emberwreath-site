# Scolen

*The lower gate cracked before dawn. By sunrise, half the canal district was awake, hauling stone, rope, and timber through the rain. At the center of it all stood Belmar of Eastmere Gate, antennae still as reeds, one hand pressed to the old flood marks carved into the wall. She did not shout. She only said, “This failed once before. We remember how to hold it.”*

## At a glance

- **Homeland:** Caelmere.
- **Known for:** crater lakes, reservoirs, canals, archives, hatchery sanctuaries, public duty, water law, civic memory, and systems that outlast their builders.
- **Common places to start:** gates, basins, locks, archive courts, hatchery shores, bridgeheads, reservoir roads, repair yards, and canal districts of Caelmere.
- **Character hooks:** Reservoir guard, canal-road scout, hatchery warden, archive investigator, flood-breaker, wandering surveyor, civic reciter, apprentice seeking a name, or Scolen seeking restitution.

## Keepers of Water and Memory

The Scolen are a many-legged people of crater lakes, reservoirs, canals, archives, and public duty. Their oldest communities rose around the waters of Caelmere, where survival depends on maintenance, memory, and trust.

Scolen are civic stewards whose lives are shaped by water that must be measured, stored, released, protected, and remembered. Their greatest works are not monuments to rulers, but systems that still function generations after their builders are gone: canals cut through old stone, reservoir roads, floodgates, terrace walls, hatchery sanctuaries, archive courts, and carved flood marks that teach the living where the water rose before.

Among Scolen, responsibility is rarely abstract. A name in an archive may decide who must repair a gate. A mentor’s lesson may outlive the mentor by decades. No generation is trusted to spend what the next will need.

## Chitin, Antennae, and Many Legs

A Scolen has a long, low, segmented lower body supported by eight walking legs arranged in four pairs. From the front rises an upright torso with two slim arms and careful three-fingered hands. Their bodies often measure 6 to 8 feet from front to rear, though the upright torso usually stands only about as high as a short human.

Their chitin is light and matte rather than heavy or armorlike. Common colors include clay gray, reed brown, wet slate, pale shell, amber, moss green, and soft blue-gray. Many Scolen polish their chitin carefully, not for vanity alone, but for health, comfort, and public presentation. Rinsing, scraping, oiling, and polishing occupy the role that bathing does among many other peoples.

Long antennae rise from a Scolen’s head and constantly read the nearby world. Through scent, vibration, pressure, and tiny movements in air or water, Scolen notice things that others miss: a footfall on a bridge, a leak behind stone, a tremor in a gate-chain, or the shift of someone standing too close. Their antennae do not make them all-seeing, but they give them a close and practical awareness of their surroundings.

Despite their water-centered homeland, adult Scolen are poor swimmers. Their earliest life is aquatic, but their adult form is built for land, wet masonry, canal paths, stairs, docks, bridges, and reservoir walls. They are steady near water, not graceful within it.

## Clothing and Equipment

Scolen clothing is built around movement, tools, and civic work. They favor draped body-cloaks, torso mantles, fitted harnesses, leg bands, side satchels, waterproof map cases, tool loops, waxed document tubes, and badges of office or apprenticeship.

Their body shape affects armor, packs, saddles, and fitted gear. Armor and clothing must be adapted for a long, many-legged frame, with space for the upright torso, walking legs, and abdomen. In communities familiar with Scolen bodies, this is ordinary work and does not normally change the cost. In foreign settlements, rushed commissions, or highly specialized cases, extra time or cost may be needed at the DM’s discretion.

Scolen use weapons, shields, tools, spellcasting focuses, and magic items normally when those items are fitted to their hands and harnesses. They do not fit ordinary humanoid saddles well. When mounted travel is necessary, they usually rely on platform saddles, side-slung frames, howdahs, carts, or custom harnesses.

## Homeland and Ecology

Caelmere is the heart of Scolen life. Its crater lakes, reservoir basins, terrace farms, canal neighborhoods, hatchery sanctuaries, archives, and old stone roads form a landscape where natural water and public engineering are inseparable. The island is home to many peoples, but Scolen institutions remain deeply woven into its civic life.

The crater lakes feed canals, refill cisterns, support terrace agriculture, preserve hatchery ecosystems, and regulate entire districts. Their levels are watched, recorded, debated, and inherited. A dry season, flood year, cracked spillway, blocked channel, or disputed release can become a public crisis.

Because of this, Scolen settlements tend to grow around functional places: gates, basins, locks, archive courts, hatchery shores, bridgeheads, reservoir roads, and repair yards. Beauty matters, but endurance matters more. A Scolen-built district is often recognized by carved gauge marks, stone channels, sheltered work crews, and walls repaired so carefully that the patch is meant to be read by future hands.

## Society, Communities, and Values

Caelmere is not a Scolen kingdom. It is a mixed island society of lake towns, terrace farms, markets, canals, sanctuaries, archives, and civic houses. Still, many of its oldest institutions grew from Scolen practice, especially the Lake Trusts and the offices that maintain public water.

Lake Trusts oversee the systems that keep communities alive. Water Stewards hold particular respect within these trusts. They decide when water may be released, which walls must be repaired first, which districts owe labor, and what records must be preserved before memory fails.

A good steward is not judged only by the crisis they survive. They are judged by whether someone else can continue the work after them. To leave no trained apprentice, no clear record, and no witness to what was done is a grave failure.

Scolen civic life also includes cohort houses, archive courts, canal judges, hatchery wardens, reservoir guards, surveyors, basin recorders, and repair crews. Their duties overlap and sometimes clash, but these disputes are not empty procedure. When water is at stake, a delayed judgment or missing record can decide the fate of a district.

Scolen can be warm, loyal, stubborn, proud, affectionate, and ambitious. Their calm manner is often mistaken for coldness, but their restraint comes from living in systems where panic wastes time and imprecision kills. They value people who can be trusted under pressure.

## Life Cycle and Family Structure

Scolen begin life in protected hatchery-lakes. Females lay eggs in a few suitable waters on Caelmere, where the young live as aquatic larvae before emergence. These hatchery-lakes are guarded as public sanctuaries and fragile ecosystems, protected from pollution, draining, and careless disturbance.

Individual eggs and larvae are not raised by parents. Many never survive to emergence, and Scolen do not know their biological parents. Those who emerge from the water, usually around age 10 to 12, enter landgoing society in cohorts. Their first years are spent learning speech, tools, movement on land, public duties, and the customs of their district.

Around age 15, a young Scolen chooses an adult name before witnesses, often after a first public task, apprenticeship trial, or act of recognized service. Training usually continues until about age 18 or 20. Scolen lives are shorter than those of many neighboring peoples, commonly 45 to 65 years, so knowledge must be passed on early and clearly.

To a Scolen, family is rarely bloodline. Cohort-mates, mentors, witnesses, apprentices, and companions form the bonds that matter most. A Scolen may call a cohort-mate sibling, a mentor kin, or an adventuring companion closer than any house of origin.

## Adventurers

Scolen adventurers often leave Caelmere because something must be repaired, recovered, proven, or understood. A Lake Trust may send one abroad to study foreign canals, negotiate water rights, recover missing records, inspect distant cisterns, or investigate forged maintenance claims. Others leave to protect hatchery waters from poison, blight, monsters, or sabotage.

Some Scolen travel because they failed. A cracked gate, lost ledger, disputed release, drowned work crew, or broken oath may send a Scolen into the wider world seeking restitution. Others flee expectations that felt too narrow, refusing to become the steward, judge, archivist, or engineer their mentors expected.

Scolen also make excellent adventurers for more personal reasons. Drowned ruins beneath Caelmere’s lakes, sealed cistern chambers, missing cohorts, old survey maps, and dangerous waterworks all call for people who can act as well as remember. A Scolen character might be a reservoir guard, canal-road scout, hatchery warden, archive investigator, flood-breaker, wandering surveyor, civic reciter, or apprentice determined to earn a name no cohort house can assign.

## How Other Peoples View Scolen

Other peoples often trust Scolen with water, records, and difficult repairs. In many mixed settlements, a Scolen surveyor or steward can calm a crowd simply by arriving with the right ledger, measuring cord, and crew. Their reputation for patience and continuity makes them welcome where canals, wells, bridges, cisterns, and floodgates matter.

That trust can become resentment. Farmers may see Scolen as the people who close a gate during drought. Merchants may curse them for delaying a road until a bridge is safe. Local leaders may dislike archives that remember old promises too clearly. In places unfamiliar with their bodies, Scolen also draw stares, awkward accommodations, and careless assumptions that they are insectlike, emotionless, or bound to procedure for its own sake.

Those who know Scolen well usually learn a sharper truth: they are not devoted to rules because rules are sacred. They are devoted to memory because forgetting has consequences.

## Names

Scolen do not inherit family names. In youth, they are known by cohort designations, caretaker nicknames, or practical identifiers. Upon entering adult society, each Scolen chooses a personal name before witnesses. In formal use, a Scolen may add a cohort, basin, civic house, apprenticeship, or public title.

Scolen names favor soft consonants, open vowels, and two- or three-syllable shapes. Common sounds include B, V, L, M, R, Z, and soft C sounds. Harsh gutturals, clipped endings, apostrophes, and chittering sounds are uncommon.

**Scolen Names:** Arel, Belmar, Cevra, Ebril, Ilvar, Lomar, Mavel, Merra, Olvar, Relin, Semra, Velis, Zoren, Zovra

**Cohort Names:** Reedmark, Lowmere, Stillbasin, Eastgate, Deepwell, Rainstep, Siltpath, Mirrorlake, Bronzegate, Floodcarve

**Civic Titles:** Water Steward, Spillwright, Hatchery Warden, Canal Judge, Archive Keeper, Terrace Surveyor, Reservoir Guard, Sluice Keeper, Basin Recorder, Floodmark Witness

Examples include Velis of Reedmark Cohort, Belmar of the Third Basin, Mavel, West Sluice Keeper, or simply Ebril.

## Scolen Traits

Your Scolen character has traits shaped by a lake-born life cycle, many-legged body, and culture of civic memory.

- **Ability Score Increase.** Your Intelligence score increases by 2, and your Wisdom score increases by 1.
- **Age.** Scolen spend their earliest years as aquatic larvae in protected hatchery-lakes, usually emerging into landgoing society around age 10 to 12. They choose adult names around age 15 and often remain apprentices until 18 to 20. Scolen commonly live 45 to 65 years, with rare individuals reaching 70.
- **Size.** Scolen adults are usually 6 to 8 feet long from front to rear, though their upright torsos rise only to about the height of a short human. Your size is Medium.
- **Speed.** Your base walking speed is 30 feet.
- **Creature Type.** You are a Humanoid.
- **Many-Legged Stability.** While you are on solid ground and not incapacitated, you have advantage on ability checks and saving throws you make to avoid being knocked prone, shoved, or moved against your will. In addition, moving across narrow, slick, or uneven constructed surfaces, such as bridges, docks, stairs, canal walls, spillways, and paved stonework, does not cost you extra movement.
- **Antennal Awareness.** You have advantage on Wisdom (Perception) checks made to notice movement, vibration, scent, or shifting air within 10 feet of you, provided your antennae are not restrained, covered, or otherwise impaired. This trait does not grant blindsight and does not allow you to automatically detect hidden or invisible creatures.
- **Civic Memory.** You gain proficiency in one of the following of your choice: History, Investigation, cartographer’s tools, navigator’s tools, mason’s tools, carpenter’s tools, calligrapher’s supplies, or tinker’s tools. In addition, when you make an Intelligence (History) check related to public works, water law, archives, reservoirs, canals, masonry, civic records, or maintenance traditions, you can add a d4 to the roll.
- **Lakeborn Body.** You can hold your breath for up to 15 minutes. You do not have a swimming speed, and you have disadvantage on Strength (Athletics) checks made to swim. In rough water, strong currents, open sea, deep water, or water where you cannot brace against a surface, you cannot make meaningful progress without magic, equipment, or aid.
- **Unusual Frame.** Armor, clothing, packs, saddles, and other fitted equipment must be adapted for your many-legged body. This does not normally change cost in communities familiar with Scolen bodies, but rare, rushed, foreign, or highly specialized work may cost more at the DM’s discretion. You use weapons, shields, tools, spellcasting focuses, and magic items normally when they are adapted for you. You do not fit ordinary humanoid saddles well and usually require a platform saddle, side-slung frame, howdah, cart, or custom harness.
- **Languages.** You can speak, read, and write Common and Scolen. Scolen is a low, flowing language shaped by precise vowels, civic terminology, and subtle antenna movements that outsiders often miss.
