# Aumari

*The low road was gone by noon. Iru of Salt-Hollow pressed one formed hand to the reef, tasted the water through their skin, and drew back. “Not this channel,” they said. “The tide has cut it deeper than it looks. We wait, or we lose someone.”*

## At a glance

- **Homeland:** Tidefall Atolls
- **Known for:** soft brine-gel bodies, compression, chemical sense, tide knowledge, clean water discipline, route memory, and yielding without losing form.
- **Common places to start:** Tidefall reef flats, brine pools, shell ridges, port districts, farm islands, lake towns, canal districts, construction camps, ships, and mixed-island neighborhoods.
- **Character hooks:** shelter repairer, cistern cleanser, brine room supporter, Rooted expectation-breaker, Diaspora worker, tide knowledge seeker, substance tester, route reader, water inspector, or identity-seeking traveler.

## People of the Shifting Margin

The Aumari are soft-bodied people of reef flats, brine pools, shell ridges, and changing tides. Their bodies are made of living brine-gel held within a flexible outer membrane, able to compress, cling, taste contamination, and reshape within careful limits. They are people of the margin, where land appears, disappears, and must be trusted only by those who know how to read it.

Their oldest ways come from the Tidefall Atolls, where a safe path at dawn may be a drowned channel by noon. Aumari culture prizes patience, emergency competence, clean water, route memory, and the discipline of yielding without losing form.

## Brine, Membrane, and Shape

Aumari have soft, semi-translucent bodies with no fixed skeleton, scales, chitin, fur, hair, or ordinary skin. Their clear outer membrane is elastic, faintly tacky, and slightly opalescent, enclosing a mass of living brine-gel. Their color comes from within: pale blue, sea-glass green, misty teal, smoky lavender, pearl rose, faint amber, moon-white, or other muted internal hues. Suspended mineral flecks, cloudy organ-masses, and slow currents may move beneath the surface.

An Aumari’s resting shape is usually low, broad, and upright, with a raised front or upper body and a wider lower mass. They can form temporary support limbs for walking, climbing stairs, bracing on a boat deck, or crossing uneven stone. Some shape two simple walking limbs, others three or four, depending on terrain and habit.

Aumari can form up to two manipulator limbs at a time. These limbs may taper, firm, widen, or split slightly at the end for gripping tools, weapons, ropes, or vessels. When speaking with other peoples, many form a stable expression area: a small mouth-fold, raised membrane patch, or cluster of primitive visual organs. Their faces are chosen shapes, not fixed features.

Aumari read brightness, contrast, motion, texture, and shape far better than color or distant detail. Among themselves, they communicate emotion through posture, ripples, scent, surface tension, opacity, vibration, and formed features. Their voices are often wet, humming, fluted, bubbly, or resonant.

Their strongest sense is chemical. Rot, brine imbalance, spoiled water, bodily scent, mineral traces, stagnant pools, and many kinds of contamination speak clearly to them by contact or close exposure.

## Clothing and Equipment

Aumari rarely wear fitted clothing. They favor flexible harnesses, seaweed-fiber netting, shell fasteners, membrane bands, waterproof pouches, sling packs, brine vessels, route charms, and loose wraps that shift as the body compresses. Diaspora Aumari adapt the same body-rigging logic with cloth, buckles, guild badges, padding, tool loops, or island-specific work gear.

Armor must be shaped around the body rather than forced onto it. Light flexible armor can be adapted with little trouble. Rigid armor limits compression. Aumari can use weapons, shields, tools, spellcasting focuses, and magic items normally when those items are held, worn, or adapted for their bodies.

## Homeland and Ecology

The Tidefall Atolls are fragile reef rings, drowned volcanoes, coral ridges, shell flats, and narrow stone rises barely lifted above the sea. At high tide, life contracts into elevated shelters, reef-anchored platforms, brine chambers, and protected cistern houses. At low tide, the world opens into temporary roads, tidal pools, seaweed fields, shellfish beds, fish traps, and exposed reef paths.

Aumari settlements are built around water discipline. Cisterns are guarded, sealed, cleaned, and watched. Brine pools are tested. Route markers are handled carefully, since moving one stone can turn a crossing into a trap. Shelters are raised above flood lines, anchored into reef or stone, and stocked for sudden isolation.

The atolls cannot support all Aumari descendants. Many now live beyond Tidefall in ports, farm islands, lake towns, canal districts, construction camps, ships, and mixed-island neighborhoods. Still, Tidefall remains the root of Aumari identity, even for those who know it only through family stories, old names, brine vessels, or elder phrases.

## Society, Communities, and Values

Aumari authority is practical rather than formal. Rooted Aumari still living in Tidefall gather in small survival bands with little standing government. Reputation, custom, place-knowledge, and emergency competence carry more weight than title. A route reader may lead when a crossing turns dangerous. A brine tester may be obeyed when water tastes wrong. A shelter hand may organize supplies during a storm. When the danger passes, authority settles back into ordinary life.

The oldest Aumari rules are simple: read the tide, protect the cistern, do not foul a pool, do not move a route marker carelessly, share emergency shelter, harvest only what the reef can recover, and never mistake comfort for safety.

The distinction between Rooted and Diaspora Aumari matters deeply. Some Rooted Aumari view the diaspora as softened, scattered, or forgetful. Some Diaspora Aumari see departure as the most Aumari choice possible: adaptation continued. Families often contain both views, and many Aumari move between those worlds over a lifetime.

## Life Cycle and Family Structure

Aumari have no biological sex and no native concept of gender. Among themselves, they do not divide bodies, roles, or kinship into male and female categories. Individual Aumari living among other peoples may adopt outside pronouns, titles, clothing cues, or presentation for personal, social, or practical reasons.

Aumari young are not born in the mammalian sense. When two Aumari choose to create a child, each contributes living gel and mineral-rich material to a protected brine pool or prepared vessel. Over time, that material coheres into a new Aumari. In Tidefall, such pools are usually part of communal brine chambers. In Diaspora communities, they may be kept in city brine rooms, shipboard basins, family vessels, or neighborhood shelters.

Parenthood is both biological and practical. An Aumari child belongs not only to those who began them, but also to those who kept the water safe while they became themselves. Young Aumari learn to hold shape, form limbs, regulate internal balance, taste safe water, compress without panic, and interpret a world they cannot read by color. They reach maturity around age twelve.

## Adventurers

Aumari adventurers often leave home because survival is not enough. Some seek coin to repair a shelter, cleanse a poisoned cistern, or support a struggling brine room. Some flee Rooted expectations. Some are Diaspora workers tired of being valued only as useful bodies. Others travel to recover tide knowledge, test dangerous substances, or decide what Aumari identity means beyond Tidefall.

They can belong to any class. Their bodies may shape how they fight, pray, cast spells, study, sing, or survive, but their class reflects choice, training, and circumstance.

## How Other Peoples View Aumari

Other peoples often respect Aumari usefulness before they understand Aumari personhood. Aumari are valued in dock work, ship repair, cistern maintenance, canal labor, fisheries, disaster cleanup, fermentation, spoilage inspection, and contamination work. This brings opportunity, but also condescension.

Axali courts may find Aumari bodies unsettling, while Scolen engineers respect Aumari water sense but often try to formalize it. Ferren crews value them on wet rope and slick stone. Humans of Breadbasket rely on them in granaries, mills, irrigation repair, and food preservation, though not always with equal status.

Aumari know this pattern well. Many are welcomed in a crisis and overlooked afterward.

## Names

Aumari personal names are usually short, soft, vowel-forward, and resonant. In formal settings, they may add a shelter, brine pool, vessel, route, profession, or earned tide-name.

**Aumari Names:** Aulo, Ei, Hama, Iru, Luma, Mau, Miri, Nalo, Nai, Omel, Oru, Reiva, Rin, Ruvo, Sui, Ulo, Velu, Vun, Yari

**Shelter, Pool, and Vessel Names:** Blue Vessel, Broken Ring, Floodmark, Hollow Shell, Lowpool, Moonflat, Pearl Basin, Raincut, Salt-Hollow, Seven Pools, Shell-Cradle, Third Cistern

**Earned Names and Titles:** Brine-Clear, Cistern Hand, Drift-Return, Kelp-Hand, Low-Tide Guide, Rainwaiter, Reef Listener, Route Reader, Salt-Taster, Shellmender, Stormheld

Examples include Miri of Lowpool, Oru Shellmender, Ei of Blue Vessel, or Rin Salt-Taster.

## Aumari Traits

Your Aumari character has traits shaped by a soft brine-gel body, tidal homeland, chemical perception, and careful internal balance.

- **Ability Score Increase.** Your Constitution score increases by 2, and your Dexterity score increases by 1.
- **Age.** Aumari reach maturity around age 12 and often live 50 to 80 years. Their bodies repair minor wear well, but long-term drying, contamination, or repeated fluid imbalance can shorten their lives.
- **Size.** Aumari are usually low, broad, and compressible rather than tall. Their exact shape varies, but they occupy about as much space as other Medium peoples. Your size is Medium.
- **Speed.** Your base walking speed is 25 feet.
- **Creature Type.** You are a Humanoid.
- **Amorphous Frame.** Your body can compress, flatten, and reshape within limits. You can move through a space as narrow as 1 foot wide without squeezing, provided you are not wearing medium or heavy armor and are not carrying rigid or bulky gear that cannot compress with you. Moving this way costs 1 extra foot of movement for every foot moved.

  You cannot end your turn in a space too small to hold your normal body shape unless the space is safe and stable enough for you to remain compressed. While compressed this way, you cannot make weapon attacks, use a shield, perform somatic components, or manipulate objects unless the space allows you to form a functional manipulator limb.

  This trait does not allow you to pass through bars, keyholes, tiny cracks, sealed doors, or spaces narrower than 1 foot.

  You can form up to two functional manipulator limbs at a time. You may also shape your lower body into simple support limbs for walking, climbing stairs, bracing, or crossing uneven ground. These support limbs do not grant extra actions, extra object interactions, extra attacks, increased reach, climbing speed, or the ability to hold additional items.
- **Clinging Body.** Your flexible body can adhere to wet stone, reef, wood, rope, and similar surfaces. You have advantage on Strength (Athletics) and Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made to avoid slipping, being washed away, or losing footing on wet, slick, tidal, or reef surfaces. In addition, wet natural surfaces do not count as difficult terrain for you.
- **Chemical Body.** Your body can detect chemical traces by contact or close exposure. You have advantage on Wisdom (Perception) and Wisdom (Survival) checks that rely primarily on smell, taste, dissolved traces, rot, brine, toxins, spoiled water, bodily scent, or chemical residue. This trait does not automatically identify specific poisons, detect magic, or replace tool proficiency.
- **Colorless Sight.** You cannot distinguish color and perceive the world through brightness, contrast, shape, motion, and texture rather than hue. You have disadvantage on Wisdom (Perception) checks made to identify color or read fine visual details beyond 30 feet.
- **Brine Balanced.** You can safely drink brackish or mildly salty water that would sicken most humanoids. You have advantage on saving throws against nonmagical illness or poison caused by spoiled, stagnant, brackish, or contaminated water.

  You cannot survive on seawater alone. If seawater is your only water source for more than 24 hours, you begin suffering dehydration normally unless you also consume freshwater, rainwater, suitable food, or treated fluids to restore your internal balance.
- **Brine Test.** By touching a liquid, food, surface, or residue for 1 minute, you can determine whether it is spoiled, stagnant, brackish, heavily contaminated, or unsafe for most humanoids to consume. This trait does not identify specific poisons, diseases, magical effects, or alchemical substances unless the DM decides the contamination would be obvious to Aumari chemical senses.
- **Unusual Frame.** You can wear armor, but rigid armor limits compression. While wearing medium or heavy armor, you cannot use Amorphous Frame to move through narrow spaces. Light flexible armor can be adapted normally.

  For game purposes, you can use weapons, shields, tools, spellcasting focuses, and magic items normally, provided they are held, worn, or adapted for your body. Magic boots and gloves usually function as membrane bands, gripping wraps, or harnessed equivalents rather than literal boots or gloves.

  You can ride standard mounts with a broad or adapted saddle, but you are uncomfortable on narrow saddles. Aumari often prefer carts, boats, sledges, sling-seats, or padded platforms.
- **Languages.** You can speak, read, and write Common and Aumari. Aumari is a soft, resonant language of open vowels, membrane vibration, breathy consonants, and subtle body-surface cues.
