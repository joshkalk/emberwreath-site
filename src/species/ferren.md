# Ferren

**Homeland:** Greenwood

The upper road is gone, the bridge posts are iced through, and the harbor bell has not answered since nightfall. So we take rope, saw, lantern, and sled. If they are alive, they are waiting for us. If they are waiting, we go.

## Children of Cold Timber

Long-bodied children of cold forest, worked timber, and winter obligation, Ferren are wiry mustelid people shaped by the conifer woods, wet harbors, and steep crossings of Greenwood. Their claws carry them across bark, rigging, rope lines, roof beams, masts, and storm-broken timber. Their dense coats shed rain and sleet, their tails steady them on narrow spans, and their hands are as comfortable with carving knives and climbing knots as with bows, axes, and tools.

Ferren are not gentle keepers of untouched wilderness. They cut, hunt, build, smoke, repair, haul, preserve, and provision. Greenwood’s forests become masts, beams, pitch, barrels, halls, docks, ropeways, crates, sleds, and winter shelters. A grove that is wasted is shameful. A bridge that fails from neglect is worse. Ferren responsibility is measured in usable wood, open routes, stocked hearths, and people brought home before the cold takes them.

Outsiders often know Ferren as climbers, timber workers, hunters, scouts, rescuers, and severe practical companions. A Ferren may not say much about loyalty, but they notice who is cold, who is hungry, who has no way back, and which promises will fail when the weather turns.

Ferren are upright mustelid humanoids with the long bodies, narrow muzzles, rounded ears, and climbing grace of Greenwood’s forest hunters. They usually stand between 4½ and 5½ feet tall, though their elongated bodies, substantial tails, and forward-ready posture often make them seem longer than they are tall. Their limbs are compact, strong, and quick, built for balance, climbing, hauling, and sudden movement across difficult ground.

A Ferren’s face is plainly nonhuman: a narrow muzzle, rounded ears, visible whiskers, dark nose, and alert eyes commonly colored brown, amber, hazel, or deep green. Their expressions are carried as much through stillness, tail position, ear angle, and posture as through the face.

Their hands are dexterous enough for writing, carving, knots, tools, weapons, shields, and spellcasting. Their fingers and toes end in curved climbing claws suited to bark, rope, rough timber, wet stone, and ship rigging. These claws are not fighting talons. They are grip, leverage, and survival.

Ferren fur appears in sable, black-brown, gray-brown, tawny, chestnut, russet, copper, red-brown, and winter-muted gray. Many have lighter throat or chest markings, darker limbs, ringed tails, or warmer underfur. Their coats are dense and weather-resistant, often marked by rain, sawdust, pitch, smoke, salt spray, or the pressed lines of harness and pack straps.

## Clothing and Equipment

Ferren dress for cold, wet weather and constant movement. Their clothing usually combines fitted wool, treated hide, leather, felt, cord, and weatherproofed cloth. Working Ferren favor reinforced harnesses, tool belts, claw-safe hand wraps, split or open climbing footwear, and short rain mantles that protect the shoulders without snagging on rope or branches.

Ferren can wear ordinary armor and clothing if it is adjusted for their tail, fur, claws, and climbing posture. They use weapons, shields, tools, spellcasting focuses, and magic items normally. Loose cloaks, long hems, and poorly fitted packs are disliked among climbers, not because Ferren cannot wear them, but because Greenwood punishes carelessness.

Their ornament is often practical: carved toggles, knife handles, combs, cloak clasps, shield grips, oath tokens, cord knots, horn fittings, or tools marked with maternal-line signs. A public Hold emblem belongs on storehouses, bridge posts, cargo marks, route stations, retainer clasps, and Warden halls.

## Greenwood

Greenwood is a cold northern island of deep evergreen forests, sheltered valleys, timber roads, steep crossings, wet harbors, and long winters. Its wealth is wood and everything wood makes possible. Straight mast trees, structural beams, pitch, charcoal, barrels, crates, sleds, bridges, carved goods, repaired docks, and ship fittings move through its harbors to the rest of the archipelago.

Ferren treat the forest as a working inheritance. They hunt game, gather eggs and mushrooms, fish streams and coasts, harvest hardy crops, cut timber, tap pitch, and preserve food against winter. A good grove is not one left untouched forever. It is one used with enough judgment that future hearths can still roof their halls, heat their shelters, repair their bridges, and feed their children.

The island’s roads and crossings matter as much as its trees. Snowed-in shelters, failed bridges, broken ropeways, blocked timber roads, and missing signal horns are not inconveniences. They are threats to life. Ferren respect the ones who keep those routes open.

## Holds, Wardens, and Hearth Obligation

Greenwood has no king. Its Ferren settlements belong to rival territories called Holds, each led by a hereditary Warden. A Hold may control a harbor, mast valley, timber road, ropeway crossing, inland village network, or chain of winter stations. Wardens collect labor, goods, service, and loyalty, but their claim is judged by performance. A Warden who cannot keep routes open, shelters supplied, people defended, and future resources protected invites resentment, desertion, or open challenge.

Ferren politics can seem tangled to outsiders because loyalty is layered. A Ferren may owe duty to a maternal line, hearth, craft crew, road station, hunting band, harbor contract, and Warden at once. These duties do not always agree. Rival Holds feud over timber rights, crossings, succession, trade marks, rescue failures, and old insults remembered because someone once froze, starved, or vanished while another Hold delayed.

Ferren values are severe but not cold-hearted. Warmth matters because winter kills. Food matters because hunger spreads. Work matters because negligence endangers others. Hospitality is not softness. A stranger taken into a hearth may be fed, dried, questioned sharply, and put to work before the hour is out.

Other peoples live throughout Greenwood, especially in harbors, mixed settlements, timber yards, and trade stations. Ferren traditions shape the old Holds, but humans, Scolen, Aumari, Axali, and others may work, trade, serve, and belong there. What they rarely do is make Greenwood simple.

## Maternal Lines and Hearths

Ferren do not recognize marriage. Romantic relationships may last a season, several years, or most of a lifetime, and they may be honored deeply by the people involved. Even so, children belong from birth to their mother’s maternal line and to the hearth that raises them.

Ferren are live-born mammals. Infants are nursed, carried, guarded from cold, and kept close during their earliest years. Most Ferren mothers bear one or two children in their lives, usually years apart, though larger hearths may raise many children through sisters, cousins, apprentices, kin, and displaced relatives.

A hearth is a circle of warmth, food, care, work, and winter responsibility. It commonly centers on a mother, her children, and maternal kin, but may include elders, long-term partners, apprentices, rescued children, sworn companions, or those who have nowhere else to go. A child’s sire may be loving, present, and respected, but the mother’s line remains the child’s permanent belonging.

Wardens may be of any gender, but Warden succession passes through maternal Ferren lines. In powerful Holds, mothers, sisters, aunts, nieces, and maternal cousins may carry great political weight.

Ferren children climb early, but they are taught that reach without judgment is dangerous. Adulthood is usually recognized when a young Ferren is trusted with responsibility that affects others: tending stores, carrying a winter message, helping mend a crossing, joining a repair crew, provisioning a route station, standing watch, assisting a hunt, or keeping a hearth supplied without supervision.

## Adventurers

Ferren leave Greenwood for duty, trade, craft, ambition, punishment, grief, politics, or escape. Some serve a Warden abroad, track stolen cargo, negotiate timber contracts, escort shipments, hunt dangerous beasts, search for lost kin, or investigate a failed shelter. Others leave because a Hold betrayed its obligations, a hearth became unsafe, a maternal-line dispute turned bitter, or the weight of everyone’s expectations grew too heavy.

A Ferren adventurer may be a Warden’s retainer, timber scout, hunter, ship rigger, bridge worker, rescuer, woodcarver, failed station survivor, oath-bound protector, traveling healer, or runaway heir. Some carry Greenwood obligation proudly. Others insist they have left it behind. Either way, Ferren tend to notice broken roofs, bad knots, empty stores, weak bridges, and abandoned people.

## How Other Peoples View Ferren

Other peoples often respect Ferren before they understand them. Sailors value Greenwood timber and trust Ferren riggers in dangerous weather. Merchants appreciate their craft goods, but complain that Ferren contracts can hinge on old Hold obligations no outsider knew existed. Travelers praise Ferren rescue parties, then remember the sharp questions that followed once they were warm enough to answer.

Scolen may admire Ferren discipline while finding their rival Holds stubborn and personal. Thryss often respect Ferren practicality, though Ferren directness can make Thryss courtesy feel evasive. Humans of Breadbasket know that Greenwood beams and barrels matter to every harvest economy. Aumari traders may find Ferren loyal once trust is earned, but slow to accept promises not backed by action. Axali often appreciate Ferren routecraft and repair work, especially where a failed bridge or dock can strand a whole settlement.

The common mistake is to treat Ferren as charming woodland folk. Ferren are not offended by warmth, music, children, or beauty, but they are shaped by harsher questions. Is the shelter stocked? Will the bridge hold? Who is missing? Who promised help? Who went?

## Names

A Ferren formally carries three names: a personal name, the name of their maternal line, and the Hold where they were raised or owe allegiance. In ordinary conversation, most use only a personal name.

Feminine names often end in -a or -e, while masculine names often end in -n, -m, or -k. These are customs, not strict rules.

**Feminine Names:** Albrin, Brakka, Cindrel, Dorna, Elda, Fritha, Hessa, Ilka, Merrow, Rilka, Tressa, Yarna

**Masculine Names:** Arven, Brek, Corm, Dallen, Edrik, Fennik, Garron, Hask, Iven, Merrik, Rusk, Vadrin

**Maternal Line Names:** Blackbough, Cinderfur, Coldhook, Darkneedle, Frostcord, Graymoss, Ironroot, Redwhisker, Sharpbeam, Snowlatch, Thornbar, Wetpine

**Hold Names:** Blackmast, Coldharbor, Firclimb, Grayspan, Hookwater, Needleroad, Pinewatch, Rainbarrow, Redbridge, Sleetford, Timberfall, Winterglen

Example full names include Ilka Frostcord Grayspan, Merrik Sharpbeam Hookwater, Dorna Blackbough Pinewatch, or simply Ilka.

## Ferren Traits

Your Ferren character has traits shaped by a long climbing frame, dense northern coat, sharp senses, and a life built around timber, hunting, and winter survival.

- **Ability Score Increase.** Your Dexterity score increases by 2, and your Strength score increases by 1.
- **Age.** Ferren reach adulthood around age 14 or 15 and commonly live 60 to 85 years.
- **Size.** Ferren are lean, long-bodied humanoids usually standing between 4½ and 5½ feet tall. Your size is Medium.
- **Speed.** Your base walking speed is 30 feet.
- **Creature Type.** You are a Humanoid.
- **Climber’s Frame.** You have a climbing speed of 30 feet.
- **Winter Coat.** You have resistance to cold damage.
- **Low-Light Eyes.** In dim light within 60 feet of you, you can see as if it were bright light. This trait does not allow you to see in darkness.
- **Timberwise.** You gain proficiency with your choice of carpenter’s tools or leatherworker’s tools. In addition, nonmagical difficult terrain created by fallen timber, logging debris, rigging, ropeways, or similar climbable wooden obstacles does not cost you extra movement.
- **Hunter’s Senses.** You have advantage on Wisdom (Survival) checks made to track creatures by scent or to find game, fish, or eggs while foraging.
- **Burdened Frame.** Your long, wiry frame is built for climbing, balance, and quick movement rather than bearing heavy loads. You count as one size smaller when determining your carrying capacity and the weight you can push, drag, or lift.
- **Climbing Claws.** Your claws are suited to grip and movement rather than combat. They do not grant you a natural weapon attack, but they are included in your Climber’s Frame and other Ferren traits.
- **Languages.** You can speak, read, and write Common and Ferren. Ferren is a clipped, practical language rich in words for routes, kinship, timber, weather, tools, debt, shelter, and obligation.
